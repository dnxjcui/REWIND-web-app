Abstract

Current AR training tools often lack accurate haptic feedback and repeatability, which limits skill development and can prolong surgical training time. Simulation reinforced with haptic feedback has been shown to help surgeons improve performance and tissue repair quality. REWIND targets a closed-loop system that combines wearable sensing, a ROS-based middleware layer, and Unity AR rendering so trainees can practice procedures with immersive visuals and timely haptic cues.

The design links a sensorized glove to an AR application through a ROS graph: joint angles from absolute magnetic encoders are published as standard JointState data, while interaction events in the scene are mapped to compact haptic commands that drive vibrotactile or force actuators. A complementary computer-vision pipeline aims to produce per-frame 3D hand and tool estimates for replay, correction, and analysis. Together, these pieces support low-latency sensing-to-render and AR-event-to-haptic paths, with modular interfaces that can scale to more fingers, sensors, or compute nodes.

Motivation

Surgical training still relies heavily on cadaver labs and learning in the operating room—approaches that are expensive, difficult to repeat, and do not always transfer cleanly to real OR performance. AR and VR simulation offer safer, repeatable alternatives, especially for minimally invasive techniques, but many AR simulators struggle with compliant soft-tissue behavior and convincing force interaction in real time. Haptic feedback is a key lever for immersivity and longer skill retention.

System overview

The system connects the glove to an AR runtime through ROS. Each finger joint is measured by AS5600 absolute magnetic encoders feeding a glove microcontroller. The MCU runs a lightweight ROS client, performs zeroing and optional filtering, and publishes joint angles. A host computer runs ROS middleware and a bridge to the AR application in Unity. The AR experience subscribes to joint angles for visualization and interaction logic; events in the scene return through ROS to the glove, where a mapper converts them into haptic commands for the actuators. Typical physical links include USB, UART, CAN, or Wi‑Fi to the ROS agent, with ROS 2 DDS on the host.

Unity scene and simulation

The Unity scene connects real-time instrument tracking with a physics-based suturing simulation. A vision pipeline can stream six-DoF instrument poses into Unity to drive a virtual needle, enabling markerless tracking that couples real motion to the simulated tool. A state machine governs needle insertion while physics constraints approximate tissue deformation, suture pull-through, and wound closure—yielding a closed loop where real-world motion produces physically grounded AR surgical behavior.

Computer vision and 3D reconstruction

We target a preprocessing-oriented pipeline that merges dynamic 3D video reconstruction with landmark detection to recover per-frame joint coordinates for wrists, fingers, and instruments—outputs intended for AR/VR replay and mechanical glove correction. The pipeline estimates camera trajectory and coarse geometry, localizes hands and infers 3D landmarks or a parametric hand model, lifts estimates into a global frame with temporal filtering and kinematic constraints, and exports joint coordinates, angles, confidence flags, and tool trajectories. Binocular capture is preferred for depth quality where feasible, with room to adopt specialized hand trackers (e.g., Dyn-HaMR-class methods) or broader scene reconstruction models as requirements firm up.

AR module

Using Microsoft HoloLens 2, the AR module estimates poses of surgical instruments and the tendon-controlled hand, organizes tool motion, finger articulation, and combined trajectories into synchronized streams, and exposes them for analysis, feedback, and logging. The module is designed for modularity: trajectory data can support path accuracy and timing metrics; integrated streams align with glove sensors for correlating kinematics with force. A feedback layer can trigger headset cues or corrective haptics while data logging enables replay and longitudinal assessment. Marker-based tracking establishes baseline accuracy first; later work explores markerless recognition and sensor fusion with explicit trade-offs between setup convenience and precision.

Haptic glove design

The glove aims for haptic feedback through twisted-string actuation (TSA), chosen for soft, biomimetic compliance. TSAs must balance motor speed and torque; a continuously variable transmission (CVT) is planned to adjust the torque ratio continuously so the glove can maintain favorable speed–torque operating points during training tasks.

Gap in existing solutions

  • Lack of integrated AR plus full-hand haptics systems
  • Limited performance evaluation and feedback within training systems
  • Lack of realistic digital hands for trainees to follow in the environment
  • Insufficient realism when combining soft tissue deformation with force interaction

Our approach: a closed-loop AR and haptic glove system that bridges this gap using joint-angle sensing, ROS-mediated haptic command mapping, and Unity AR rendering to deliver immersive, haptic-reinforced surgical training.

Next steps

  • Improve realism of haptic feedback to better mimic soft tissue behavior
  • Reduce end-to-end latency for smoother real-time interaction
  • Expand AR scenarios toward fuller surgical procedures
  • Integrate more advanced tracking and sensor fusion
  • Conduct user studies on training outcomes and usability
  • Move toward a more portable and scalable system design
